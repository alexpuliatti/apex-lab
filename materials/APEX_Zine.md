In a world of virtual overstimulation, the process 
of one’s assimilation with technological forces 
seems almost Sysiphian [impossibly challenging] 
in its complexity. With the constant online barrage 
of information and entertainment demanding to 
be accessed and viewed by its users, the Internet 
now exists as a ‘reality’ famed for its proliferating 
quality- and a perceived struggle to adapt with our 
increasingly fragmenting identities. In T echgnosis, 
Erik Davis (2015) aims to explain this  
cultural phenomenon:
Boundaries dissolve, and we drift into the 
no-man’s zones between synthetic and organic 
life, between actual and virtual environments, 
between local communities and global flows of 
goods, information, labor, and capital. With pills 
modifying personality, machines modifying bodies, 
and synthetic pleasures and networked minds 
engineering a more fluid and invented sense 
of self, the boundaries of our identities are  
mutating as well.  
(Davis and Thacker, 2015)
While this may seem overbearing, it’s important to 
further note casualties in everyday life emerging 
from political, social and economic turmoil 
from the likes of government distrust, cost-of-
living crisis, wars and genocides looming in our 
screens, and so on. With all this in mind, younger 
generations are experiencing feelings and beliefs 
of nihilism- as a coping mechanism transferred 
from the postmodern into the current meta-
modern paradigm.
But although the former’s outlook was one of 
more existential dread, the latter embraces the 
feeling as a fantastical opening that shatters the 
‘cycle of cultural reflection and moves toward 
culture-making’(MORNING, 2023) and community-
mindedness. Three years before her Sunny 
Nihilist book release, Wendy Syfret writes about 
her discovery of the nihilist perspective and its 
‘comforting revelation’:
When your existence is pointless, you shift  
focus to things that have more longevity than 
your own ego. I’ve become more engaged 
in environmental issues, my family and the 
community at large. Once you make peace with 
just being a lump of meat on a rock, you can stop 
stressing and appreciate the rock itself. 
 
(Syfret, 2019)
This mindset has arguably been a long time in the 
making, and signifies what Arquilla (2011) refers to 
as ‘a bend of History’(Arquilla, 2011).  
His notion aims to capture the increasing cultural 
awareness about the fact that we recognise there 
is something at stake without the ability to find out 
exactly what in present time- “hidden around the 
bend, as it were”(Timotheus Vermeulen, 2017)- 
but this time around, it is welcomed rather than 
feared with outstretched hands and lack of 
 pre-existing glimmers of hope.
This paper aims to explore the shift in trend 
paradigms spanning from tradition/realism and its 
convergence to modernism, then to  
post-modernism in the 1950s- to the present  
meta-modernist era we entered end of 2000s 
according to researchers (Vermeulen and 
van den Akker, 2010), in relation to Gen Z 
online users’ nihilistic affect that leads them to 
explore fantastical elements/ experiences in 
the digital sphere that (MORNING, 2023) coins 
‘MetaFantasy’. The Gen Z user demographic 
identifies as individuals born between late  
1990s-2011 who interact with communities on 
social media platforms. Finally, the reasoning 
behind nihilism’s trending ‘meaningless’ worldview 
will be discussed, as will its impact on future 
consumer culture.

The Great  
Terminology Legend  
The initial framework or ‘wireframe’ of society lies 
in the secular hands of tradition. (Dember, 2020) 
describes tradition as a ‘culture devoted to preserving 
the knowledge of the past’ whereby artists strive to 
excel in their fields based on ‘established standards’ 
not fuelled by progress so much as individuals 
leaving their mark in ways that appeases archaic 
perception. From traditional clothing and cuisine, 
to mannerisms and values- tradition emphasizes 
continuity, preservation of heritage, and fierce 
resistance to change- with slow and incremental 
cultural shifts that preserve customs and beliefs. 
The alphabet and its subsequent scriptures evoked 
a boom of information, and with the Greek alphabet 
‘becoming the first writing machine to capture vowels 
as well as consonants’(Timotheus Vermeulen, 
2017), the ‘technological colonization of the spoken 
world’ reached its completion. But as society 
procured further questions left to be answered, 
the trend paradigm of tradition no longer seemed 
suitable for the increasingly curious, scientifically 
fueled people. And so in the 18/19th century 
during the Enlightenment, we entered modernism.
TRADITION ---> MODERNISM --->
Characterised by its enthusiasm for invention, 
liberation from traditional outdated norms, and 
the urge to pursue new ideas, modernism became 
notable in the 19th century and shone a spotlight 
on individualism, experimentation, and exploration 
for universal structural truths. At this time, society’s 
overarching feeling was one of hope for what Lewis 
Mumford calls ‘myth of the machine’, insisting on 
the ‘authority of technical and scientific elites, and 
the intrinsic value of efficiency, control, unrestrained 
technological development, and economic 
expansion’(Timotheus Vermeulen, 2017). With 
tradition still being society’s wireframe, these ideals 
were all framed by Christian myths: ‘the biblical 
call to conquer nature, the Protestant work ethic’ 
and specifically the millenialist vision of an earthly 
paradise from the likes of New Jerusalem- said by the 
Book of Revelation to be the highest point in history.

POST-MODERNISM ---> META-MODERNISM ---?
Of course, this unbridled excitement to dominate 
and conquer ultimately led to power-hungry 
individuals and some of the most gruesome, 
bloodthirsty wars of the modern world. Post-
modernism emerges in post-war times, most 
prominently 1960-2000s and is driven by a 
suspicion/mistrust of universalisms and optimism 
for progress that modernism promoted(Dember, 
2020). With overarching feelings of cynicism 
and suspicion, post-modernism deconstructs 
established norms in celebration of diversity, irony, 
and parody- also making it the era where nihilism 
first emerges- courtesy of Friedrich Nietzche. Post-
war generations were the first to experience the 
Age of Internet and ‘surf’ the WorldWideWeb to 
establish communities and build neural networks.
Metamodernism is both a trend paradigm that 
reflects the current state of global capitalism and a 
structure of feeling that arises from and responds 
to post-modernism. Researchers (Vermeulen 
and van den Akker, 2010) argue that this dominant 
cultural logic of Western capitalist societies should 
be situated in the 2000s, and is characterized by 
“oscillating between a modern enthusiasm and 
post-modern irony”. This new paradigm arose 
because it feels like postmodern discourses no 
longer account for all ideals and sensibilities in 
contemporary life, and indeed have lost their ‘critical 
value’ regarding its culture, politics, and aesthetics. 
As Searle stated when discussing alter-modernism: 
“Post-modernism is dead, but something altogether 
weirder has taken its place”(Searle, 2009)- which 
can be extricated from 2000s’ wane of mass- [and 
emergence of] social media culture. (Timotheus 
Vermeulen, 2017) wrote a powerful analogy 
that summarizes the respective powers of the 
paradigms created post-tradition: “the modernists 
excavated depth from the surface, the post-
modernists flattened it by means of the surface, the 
meta-modernists apply depth onto the surface.”
Similar to how post-modernism arose when people became 
dissatisfied with modernism’s certainty, metamodernism arose 
when people became exhausted by postmodernism’s scepticism. 
(Dember, 2020)


Gen Z Users  and Existential  Nihilistic Beliefs
Nihilism as a coping mechanism:
This study aims to showcase that a prevalent concept in the meta-modernist paradigm shaping 
contemporary culture is existential nihilism. This belief cannot be defined on its own ‘nihilism’ 
as it refers to the belief in nothing, with root words deriving from the Latin ‘nihil’. Over the ages, 
it has been viewed as daunting and generally seen in a negative light. So for the purpose of the 
study, ‘nihilism’ needs to be further defined on ‘existential’ crutches.
Existential nihilism is a philosophical belief that asserts the inherent meaninglessness and lack 
of purpose in life. It suggests that life has no objective or intrinsic value, and that human existence 
is ultimately without significance. One of its notable conveyors was Friedrich Nietzche- an 
existentialist and critic of organised religion- who worried about existence being undervalued and in 
his texts expressed concern over how ‘existing ideas of reason and morality were fixated on whats 
next’(Syfret, 2022). As a coping mechanism, it can be seen as a way for individuals to confront the 
existential anxieties and uncertainties of life by embracing the idea that nothing ultimately matters 
(Syfret, 2022).
Creating subjective meanings  
and value systems:
In a world where the quest for meaning has been commercialized and exploited for reasons 
benefiting capitalism and economies- at the expense of societal well-being, ‘sunny’ existential 
nihilism offers a reprieve and humourful approach with ironic undertones. Capitalism itself can 
be defined as a political ideology adopted by Western cultures that runs on human labour to 
generate profit for a country’s GDP . Subjective meanings and value systems are created in the 
21st century that differ from post-war because of GenZ’s capacity of using their nihilistic beliefs 
to inspire action, not apathy: “Because before you can be truly motivated to imagine a new 
world, you have to totally lose faith in the old one”(Syfret, 2022).

Detachment from societal expectations:
From a post-modernist perspective, existential nihilism aligns with the scepticism towards 
grand narratives and absolute truths. The paradigm first experiences this void-filled thinking 
and ensues detachment from societal expectations, where post-modernists reject the notion of 
a fixed and universal meaning in life, emphasizing the subjective and fragmented nature of  
human experiences. 
However, metamodernism offers a more nuanced approach to existential nihilism.  
By recognizing the inherent tensions and contradictions within contemporary society,  
it acknowledges both the absurdity and the potential for meaning-making. It suggests that 
individuals can engage with existential nihilism as a starting point for personal growth and 
self-reflection, while also striving to create new narratives and value systems that transcend 
the limitations of nihilism. Wendy Syfret refers to this phenomenon as ‘Sunny Nihilism’ where 
she harnesses its power (or lack of) and urges readers to channel it to their everyday lives as 
a ‘blank page’: “a chance to enjoy the moment, the present, the chaos and luck of being alive at 
all.”(Syfret, 2022).

The Power  of Metafantasy
Definition and concept  of Metafantasy:
In present socio-political/ economic environments, people 
are looking to escape their reality by engaging with fantastical 
elements. Metafantasy is a concept in social media that refers 
to the creation and engagement with fantastical hyper-realistic 
elements in the digital sphere. It involves the act of constructing 
and participating in elaborate narratives and storylines that often 
blur the lines between reality and fantasy (hyperrealism), allowing 
individuals to explore different identities and experiences. 
Metafantasy can be seen as a form of escapism and self-
expression, where users immerse themselves in a world of their 
own creation, shaping and reshaping their online presence 
through the characters they embody.
Baudrillard's hyperreality:
French sociologist Jean Baudrillard (1981) defined 
‘hyperreality’ as “the generation by models of a real 
without origin or reality”(Mambrol, 2016) in Simulacra 
and Simulation. Although it’s not a relatively new 
concept, it’s interesting to notice its effects more 
relevant today than during its initial conceptualization. 
This is because it did a good job of capturing the 
postmodern situation, in particular how people in 
postmodernity create fake worlds of spectacle and 
seduction in order to get stimulation, but nothing 
more. Hyperreality is characterised as a state in 
which the boundaries between reality and fiction are 
blurred, making it difficult to tell where one stops and 
the other begins. It permits the blending of artificial 
intelligence (AI) and human intellect with virtual reality 
(VR) and physical reality, and it’s especially prevalent in 
technologically advanced post/meta-modern  
societies (Mambrol, 2016).

Construction of alternative  realities and narratives:
Researcher Shaun Wilson argues that ‘magic’ is a term that in 
contemporary life has moved away from its semantic history to become 
a mechanism that ‘prescribes both ontological and epistemological 
assessments of meta-modern affect’ to experience culture (Wilson, 
2023). This is relevant to alternative reality and narrative construction 
in social media because people are seeking magical experiences and 
exaggerated mysticism as a form of entertainment, hope, and forming 
new connections. Erik Davis said that ‘spiritualism was the first popular 
religion of the information age’ (Davis and Thacker, 2015) and reflects 
on the popstars of the Victorian Era- whereby the transformation of a 
communications medium turned into a consumer spectacle (1860/70s) 
in an effort to exaggerate and capitalize off the entertainment.  
This phenomenon is common today with the use of AI and spiritual 
trends from the likes of astrology and celebration of the occult- where 
societal fragmentation aids in fantastical constructions of new  
realities online.
Themes of rebellion,  absurdity, and existentialism:
As society fragments and topics become polarised, we’re left navigating 
two worlds, living in a hell state of cognitive dissonance. How did we get 
here,what side should we choose and is choosing sides really the best 
way to move forward?  
(MØRNING, 2023)
Although the quote by MØRNING offers a more dystopian perspective, 
it opens the conversation of why themes of rebellion, absurdity, and 
existentialism are prevalent. Metafantasy offers an oscillation between 
reality and digital with magical elements that the Sartrean perspective 
identifies as ‘acts of imagination to be enacted through reality in a sense 
of totality from freedom’(Wilson, 2023). In an age of pent-up angst 
and general community distrust, these themes offer a magical coping 
alternative to individuals by offering a platform to challenge norms, 
embrace the nonsensical, and delve into deep existential questions. 
They can experiment with different aspects of themselves, grapple with 
their own mortality, and contemplate the nature of reality. The fluidity 
and malleability of the metafantasy environment mirror the existentialist 
notion of self-creation and the search for personal authenticity.

Impact on Future Consumer Culture
Influence of Gen Z users  on cultural landscape:
In the realm of metafantasy, Gen Z users bring unique perspectives and 
creative expressions borne from their digital fluency/ability to navigate 
and shape online spaces, making them a contemporary driving force. 
They embrace platforms to explore identities, challenge societal norms, 
and create content that resonates with their generation. 
Their engagement with metafantasy further blurs the lines between 
reality and fiction, as they actively participate in the creation and 
dissemination of narratives. Gen Z users have a profound influence on 
the cultural landscape through their activism, preferences, and  
creative expressions.
Commodification of existential nihilism in online spaces:
In online spaces, brands and marketers capitalize on Gen Z nihilist 
appeal by creating and promoting products that evoke a sense of 
authenticity, individuality, and nonconformity, appealing to the desires 
of Gen Z consumers who seek to challenge societal norms and explore 
alternative ways of being. It shapes consumer preferences, values, and 
experiences, influencing the entertainment industry and other cultural 
spheres. While it provides opportunities for creative expressions and 
authentic experiences, there is a need to ensure the integrity and depth 
of existential themes in the face of commercialization.

Problems arise when the promises and expectations tied to meaning begin 
to eclipse the concept itself, which I would argue is exactly where we find 
ourselves today. Somewhere along the line that noble, deeply personal, perhaps
lifelong quest began to feel more urgent and commodified, the pursuit of meaning
shifted from an epic journey to a scavenger hunt. It’s not enough to try and locate purpose in 
love, family, work or religion. Now we’re being asked to find meaning in everything we do.  
(Syfret, 2022)
Research findings allude to the following argument: It seems that the mass-
commodification of ‘meaning’ as a concept has forced younger demographics like Gen 
Z to adopt an existentialist nihilistic attitude. The demographic may use this as a form 
of humourful (meta)irony to cope with the absurdity of capitalism. It’s important to note 
that in this case overarchingly Western societies affected by this ideology are being 
discussed. What makes this such an interesting phenomenon is that Gen Z is the first 
generation to grow up with technology having widespread reach to information, and as 
supported by my interview with Gen Z music artist Alma: “its enabled us to form more 
critical and sceptical outlooks on life, because we’ve just had access to more [data]”. 
But the big problem arises when ‘meaning’ is used as a marketing form to sell products 
to the masses, thereby depreciating the value of the word itself. As supported by The 
Extreme Self, “Its not enough to have virtue anymore. Y ou need to signal it to show 
you have it.”(Shumon Basar et al., 2021), So moving forward, how can we as a society 
consciously develop when faced with such a [moral] obstacle?
What this signifies for culture-making can be a liberating approach to self-expression online, 
community-building, and greater sustainability improvements, but also runs the risk of 
leading to disorientation and existential dread affecting the modern mind.

Sunny Nihilism:
This study aims to showcase that positive/‘sunny’ nihilism can be a favourable 
impact for future consumer culture. When answering the research question, I 
came upon author Wendy Syfret who rephrases ‘existential dread’ into something 
I find more relatable- ‘fear of pointlessness’. Syfret argues, “pointlessness is a 
state we’ve been conditioned to distrust…Everything we do needs a point, a 
focus, a goal, in an attempt to serve as one more step towards a level of happiness 
and success. But as this book argues, its a false promise. So, when ‘finding a 
point’ makes you miserable, an alternative emerges: embracing the pleasure 
of pointlessness.”(Syfret, 2022). As an individual raised in a purely capitalistic 
period, I had to be a bit more open-minded when reading this because it’s been so 
ingrained that the definition of success is a steady well-paying job and materialistic 
benefits. As a Gen Z myself, I often struggle with the fear of pointlessness and 
how what is expected of me in society can lead me down a path that forsakes 
my creativity and pleasure for that of mundanity and profit. But in the modern 
world, where a collective loss of faith in pre-existing structures is evident, how 
can Gen Z pick up the baton left by the past generation and move forward in a 
community-minded and sustainable manner? Facilitated by social media and 
immersive technological developments, new forms of communication and ways 
of living are being [re]invented. My interview with Alex Pulatti further supports this 
when he mentions how he’s found people with more in common online based in 
Australia (eg) than in his hometown Sicily. In my opinion, sunny nihilism is a great 
way to continue the movement as it liberates Gen Z from generational guilt and 
constructs that may no longer fit as well in society as they did before, paving the 
way for a less obstructive future consumer culture.

Conclusion:
T o conclude, this research aimed to explore why GenZ’s adoption of existential nihilistic 
beliefs may be used as a coping mechanism to deal with everyday life, and how future 
consumer culture is consequently impacted. It appears that younger generations, such 
as Gen Z, are compelled to embrace an existentialist nihilistic mindset due to the mass-
commercialization of the concept of “meaning”, generally caused by capitalistic ideologies. 
What this means for culture-making can be a freeing way to express oneself online, foster 
community, and achieve larger sustainability goals, but it also carries the risk of confusing 
people and creating existential dread that affects the contemporary mind. Tim Dowdall 
PhD labels this as ‘disorientation’ and I discovered three subsequent causalities affecting 
consumer culture: passive thought, meta-irony, and sunny nihilism. I chose to focus on the last 
two due to greater relevance to the research question, as I aimed to determine their affect.
Metamodernism gave rise to the phenomena of sunny/positive nihilism (Syfret, 2022), 
which provides a blank slate for critical thinkers. Nothing prevents you from discovering your 
own meaning in life—not cultural constraints, preconceptions, or predispositions. Meta-
irony- a meta-modern form of humour, is employed as a rebellious coping mechanism to 
combat disorientation by adding layers of irony until the audience is unable to distinguish 
between a serious and humorous claim. Both are forms of escapism hosted in Metafantasy- 
a widely-used social media concept involving fantastical, hyper-realistic elements to 
create and interact with digital content, allowing users to shape their online personas and 
self-expression. Research indicates that nihilistic and meta-ironic Gen Z attitudes have 
successfully broken barriers and emphasize the significance of access and truthfulness- 
factors held at high value for consumer culture.
Through interviews with industry professionals (some Gen Z) and literary research,  
I explored identity fragmentation, hyper-personalization, and self-cores which I believe

existential urge to storify themselves in order to deal with the breakdown of the world 
narrative. MCs reject nihilism while embracing the fluid self through meta-ironic/fantastical 
experiences, and rejecting pre-existing postmodern ideals more rigid in character.

Accelerationism:Need for speed
I first began researching this topic when I was 
having the overwhelming feeling that time 
is moving too fast for me to process. I kept 
ending up at this crossroad: Am I being forced 
to acclimatize to the rapidly changing ‘digital

In all honesty, I think It’s probably a bit of both. 
But accelerationism is a process that has been 
occurring since the beginning of the Industrial 
Revolution- since man realised the ability to 
create more output at a rate faster than ever 
before- and that startling hunger only increased 
with the dawn of the Internet (and capitalism). 
Accelerationism can be defined as a futorological 
movement of thought that advocates for the rapid 
and unrestricted advancement of technology 
as a means to bring about radical societal 
change. It posits that the existing systems 
of capitalism and societal structures are not 
sustainable in the long term, and that the way 
to transcend these is to accelerate the pace of 
technological development, rather than resisting 
it. Accelerationists believe that this approach 
will force society to adapt and evolve, ultimately 
leading to a post-capitalist society. 
”The label has only been in regular use since 2010, 
when it was borrowed from Zelazny’s novel by 
Benjamin Noys, a strong critic of the movement- 
“accelerationists are interested in some of the 
following factors of 21st century: what it means to 
be human in an era of addictive, intrusive electronic 
devices; the seemingly uncontrollable flows of 
global markets; the power of capitalism as a 
network of desires; the increasingly blurred 
boundary between the imaginary and the factual; 
the resetting of our minds and bodies by ever-
faster music and films; and the complicity, revulsion 
and excitement so many of us feel about the speed 
of modern life.”” (Beckett, 2017) 
Nick Land is what one might call a fervent 
purveyor of accelerationism- or #father. (F . 
Wijarnarko and D. Maharani, 2024) state that, 
“Nick land through accelerationism, provides a 
view that technological development must be 
accelerated and allowed to run. Land believes 
that technology will bring humans to the next 
stage of evolution to a better life.” But the concept 
of ‘better life’ leads only to more questions: 
Andrea evoked my distrust with this when she 
said, “Is it something that I actually need, or is it 
something that our planet needs [with all its tied-
in developments]?” In my interview with Nella I 
further enquired, “But to what extent? Would it be 
a positive change? How could this be advocated 
for in a tangible sense?” 
Land’s work has also been tied to the 
development of speculative realism, a 
philosophical movement emerging in the early 
21st century as a reaction against the dominant 
philosophies of the time which were heavily 
focused on human perspective and experience. 
It asserts that reality exists independently of 
human minds and that it is possible to know 
things about this reality that aren’t filtered through 
human perception. It challenges the idea that 
human-centric understanding is at the center 
of philosophy, instead advocating for a ‘realist’ 
approach that acknowledges the existence 
of a world independent of human thought and 
interpretation. From a more moral (or ethical?) 
approach we can then be introduced to effective 
accelerationism, pushing us to harness these 
rapid advancements and steer them in a  
direction that is in line with sustainable and 
compassionate ideals.

I am obviously going to mention philosophers Deleuze & Guattari when talking about 
identity. This rhizomatic understanding of self, as described by D&G, is integral to our 
modern conception of digital identities. A rhizome (used as a metaphor), unlike a tree, 
does not have a central trunk from which branches sprout. Instead, it is a network 
of interconnected nodes, each equally important and none subordinate to another. 
This rhizomatic structure mirrors the nature of online identities, which are not linear 
or hierarchical but rather a complex web of connections and influences. The internet, 
in its vastness and complexity, functions much like a rhizome, with each user or node 
interlinked in an intricate network (Olivier, 2015). In this context, individual identities 
become fluid and interconnected, with the boundaries between self and other often 
blurred. This dissolution of boundaries is further amplified by the inherent anonymity 
and flexibility of digital spaces, where one can assume multiple identities, often 
simultaneously.

disconnected, owning nothing and everything’ is a potent reflection of this rhizomatic 
reality. It encapsulates the paradoxes inherent to digital life - the tension between

This section is inspired by Timothy Morton’s ‘Dark Ecology’(Morton, 2018) which 
presents an ecological critique of the Anthropocentric perspective, urging humanity 
to acknowledge and embrace the interconnectedness and coexistence of all life 
forms. In envisioning a futuristic ecology, Morton’s framework could guide us towards 
a harmonious balance between nature and humanity. This balance would involve 
recognizing the inherent value of all non-human entities and abandoning the hierarchical 
view that places humans at the center of the ecological equation. Instead, we would need 
to adopt a more holistic and humble understanding of our place in the ecological web, 
acknowledging that we are part of a vast, interconnected network of life.
T o achieve this harmonious coexistence, humanity would need to radically alter its 
relationship with the natural world. This would involve not only a shift in perspective but 
also tangible changes in our behaviors and systems. We would need to transition from 
exploitative practices to sustainable ones, ensuring that our actions contribute to the 
health and resilience of the ecological network. 
Furthermore, this vision of a futuristic ecology is deeply connected to effective 
accelerationism. By accelerating technological and cultural advancements, we can 
expedite the necessary societal transformations, fostering a future where humans 
and nature coexist in a state of mutual thriving. This shift towards a more ecologically 
conscious society represents an essential step in our collective evolution, aligning with 
the accelerationist notion of harnessing technology and culture to propel us into a post-
capitalist future.
And regarding the increasing fluidity of Self, as our understanding of ourselves becomes 
more interconnected and less hierarchical, mirroring the ecological web, so too does our 
understanding of ‘identity’. Fluid identities are, by nature, resistant to rigid categorization 
and instead embrace a more flexible, adaptable form of self-definition. This parallels the 
ecological perspective, which acknowledges the interdependence and diversity of life 
forms without imposing a rigid hierarchical structure. An example of this in nature would 
be the rhizome structure (haiii D&G:)).
  Looking forward, the logic of coexistence will greatly determine how we, as a society, 
navigate the integration of technological advancements into our lives. As we continue to 
develop and rely upon technology, it’s essential to ensure that this reliance fosters unity 
rather than division. This means designing and implementing technology in a way that 
respects and accommodates the fluid, interconnected nature of our identities, rather
